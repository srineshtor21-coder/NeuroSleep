Overview Epidemiology

What is Alzheimer's Disease?

Alzheimer's disease (AD) is the most common form of dementia, accounting for 60 to 80 percent of all dementia cases worldwide. It is a progressive, irreversible neurodegenerative disorder characterized by the destruction of neurons and synaptic connections, leading to severe impairment in memory, language, problem-solving, and other cognitive functions. As of 2024, more than 55 million people worldwide are living with Alzheimer's disease or another form of dementia, and this number is projected to triple by 2050 absent significant preventive intervention.

The disease progresses through three clinical stages: preclinical (no symptoms, pathological changes occurring), mild cognitive impairment (subtle symptoms), and dementia (functional impairment). Critically, amyloid-beta accumulation begins 15 to 20 years before any clinical symptoms appear, emphasizing the importance of early behavioral prevention strategies.

Alzheimer's Association. (2024). 2024 Alzheimer's Disease Facts and Figures. Alzheimer's and Dementia, 20(5).
Pathology Neuropathology

Neuropathological Hallmarks

Alzheimer's disease is defined by two primary neuropathological features: extracellular amyloid-beta plaques and intraneuronal neurofibrillary tau tangles. These accumulations disrupt synaptic transmission, activate neuroinflammatory cascades, and ultimately lead to neuronal death. The hippocampus and entorhinal cortex are affected earliest, explaining why episodic memory is the first cognitive domain to decline.

A third hallmark, neuroinflammation mediated by activated microglia and reactive astrocytes, has emerged as a critical driver of disease progression. Neuroinflammatory responses initially attempt to clear amyloid aggregates but become chronic and destructive as the pathological burden increases.

Braak staging divides AD progression into six histopathological stages based on the distribution of neurofibrillary tangles, from Stage I (entorhinal) to Stage VI (widespread neocortical involvement).
Braak, H., & Del Tredici, K. (2011). The pathological process underlying Alzheimer's disease in individuals under thirty. Acta Neuropathologica, 121(2), 171-181.
Amyloid-Beta Molecular Biology

Amyloid-Beta Cascade Hypothesis

The amyloid cascade hypothesis, first proposed by Hardy and Higgins in 1992 and substantially refined over three decades, posits that the aberrant processing and accumulation of amyloid precursor protein (APP) into amyloid-beta peptides (particularly Ab42) is the initiating event in Alzheimer's pathogenesis. The A4 protein (now known as APP) is cleaved by beta and gamma secretases to produce Ab peptides that aggregate first into soluble oligomers, then into insoluble fibrillar plaques.

Soluble amyloid-beta oligomers are now recognized as the primary toxic species, disrupting long-term potentiation, activating inflammatory pathways, and impairing synaptic plasticity even before plaque deposition becomes visible on PET imaging. This insight has redirected drug development toward oligomer neutralization strategies.

Selkoe, D.J., & Hardy, J. (2016). The amyloid hypothesis of Alzheimer's disease at 25 years. EMBO Molecular Medicine, 8(6), 595-608.
Glymphatic System Sleep Connection

The Glymphatic System: Sleep's Waste Removal Service

Discovered by Maiken Nedergaard and colleagues at the University of Rochester in 2012 and 2013, the glymphatic system is a highly organized waste clearance network that uses perivascular channels formed by astrocytic endfeet to drive cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) through brain tissue. This system is responsible for clearing metabolic waste products including amyloid-beta, tau, lactate, and other potentially neurotoxic solutes from the interstitial space.

The landmark 2013 Science paper by Xie et al. demonstrated that sleep increases interstitial space volume by approximately 60 percent, dramatically enhancing convective CSF flow. This means the glymphatic system operates at peak efficiency almost exclusively during sleep, and particularly during slow-wave (NREM) sleep stages. Disruption of slow-wave sleep therefore directly impairs the brain's ability to eliminate amyloid-beta and tau, potentially contributing to plaque accumulation over years and decades.

Key Finding: Amyloid-beta clearance rates are approximately 2x higher during sleep than during wakefulness. In mouse models, just one night of sleep deprivation produces measurable increases in interstitial amyloid-beta concentration.
Xie, L., Kang, H., Xu, Q., et al. (2013). Sleep drives metabolite clearance from the adult brain. Science, 342(6156), 373-377.
Sleep-AD Link Human Studies

Human Evidence: Sleep Duration and Alzheimer's Risk

A series of large prospective cohort studies have now established sleep duration and quality as independent risk factors for Alzheimer's dementia in human populations. Spira et al. (2013) found that self-reported shorter sleep duration and poorer sleep quality were associated with greater amyloid-beta burden on PET imaging, even after controlling for age, sex, and cognitive status.

The ARIC Neurocognitive Study, following over 8,000 participants for 25 years, demonstrated that sleeping 6 hours or less per night in midlife was associated with a 30 percent increased risk of developing dementia compared to sleeping 7 hours. A 2021 Nature Communications study of 8,000 UK Biobank participants found that consistent 6-hour sleep in those aged 50 to 60 was associated with a 30 percent higher dementia risk, independent of psychiatric, behavioral, cardiometabolic, and socioeconomic factors.

Bidirectional relationship: Sleep disruption accelerates Alzheimer's pathology, and accumulating amyloid-beta disrupts the sleep-wake cycle, creating a self-reinforcing degenerative loop documented by Ju et al. (2017).
Sabia, S., et al. (2021). Association of sleep duration in middle and old age with incidence of dementia. Nature Communications, 12(1), 2289.
Prevention Public Health

Modifiable Risk and Prevention Strategies

The 2020 Lancet Commission on dementia prevention, intervention, and care identified 12 modifiable risk factors collectively accounting for approximately 40 percent of worldwide dementia cases. These include: less education, hypertension, hearing impairment, smoking, obesity, depression, physical inactivity, diabetes, low social contact, excessive alcohol consumption, traumatic brain injury, and air pollution. Sleep quality, while not listed explicitly as a standalone factor, is embedded within depression, physical inactivity, and cardiovascular risk pathways.

The commission's core recommendation is population-wide lifestyle intervention beginning in midlife, underscoring the importance of platforms like NeuroSleep that make behavioral tracking accessible to young people during the critical preventive window before pathological accumulation becomes established.

Livingston, G., et al. (2020). Dementia prevention, intervention, and care: 2020 report of the Lancet Commission. The Lancet, 396(10248), 413-446.
Sleep Architecture

NREM and REM Sleep Stages

Sleep is not a homogeneous state but a structured cycle of distinct stages with specialized neurological functions. Non-rapid eye movement (NREM) sleep is subdivided into N1 (light sleep), N2 (sleep spindles and K-complexes), and N3 (slow-wave or deep sleep). Rapid eye movement (REM) sleep is characterized by dream mentation, muscle atonia, and hippocampal-neocortical memory replay. A complete sleep cycle lasts approximately 90 minutes, and the brain cycles through 4 to 6 cycles per night. N3 slow-wave sleep, dominant in the first half of the night, is the primary window for glymphatic clearance and declarative memory consolidation.

Diekelmann, S., & Born, J. (2010). The memory function of sleep. Nature Reviews Neuroscience, 11(2), 114-126.
Sleep Deprivation

Cognitive Costs of Insufficient Sleep

Van Dongen et al.'s landmark 2003 study demonstrated that restricting sleep to 6 hours per night for 14 days produces cognitive performance deficits equivalent to two full nights of total sleep deprivation, yet subjects failed to perceive their own impairment. The psychomotor vigilance task (PVT) showed progressive, dose-dependent lapses. Walker (2017) synthesized evidence showing that sleeping fewer than 6 hours impairs prefrontal cortex function, reducing working memory capacity, emotional regulation, and executive decision-making.

Van Dongen, H.P.A., et al. (2003). The cumulative cost of additional wakefulness. Sleep, 26(2), 117-126.
Memory

Sleep and Memory Consolidation

The two-stage model of memory consolidation holds that new declarative memories are initially encoded in the hippocampus during waking experience, then replayed and transferred to distributed neocortical networks during NREM sleep. Sleep spindles (12 to 15 Hz oscillations generated by the thalamic reticular nucleus) coordinate with hippocampal sharp-wave ripples to orchestrate this transfer. REM sleep additionally strengthens procedural and emotional memories. The hippocampal-neocortical dialogue during NREM sleep is now understood to be essential for long-term memory stability.

Stickgold, R. (2005). Sleep-dependent memory consolidation. Nature, 437(7063), 1272-1278.
Neurotoxins

Metabolic Waste and Brain Health

During wakefulness, neuronal metabolic activity generates waste products including amyloid-beta, tau, lactate, and reactive oxygen species. The absence of a conventional lymphatic drainage system in the brain necessitated the evolution of the glymphatic system for waste removal. Nedergaard's research established that aquaporin-4 (AQP4) water channels on astrocytic endfeet are required for efficient glymphatic flow. Mislocalization of AQP4, which occurs with aging, substantially reduces glymphatic efficiency and correlates with increased amyloid deposition.

Nedergaard, M. (2013). Garbage truck of the brain. Science, 340(6140), 1529-1530.

Chronobiology

The Circadian Clock System

The suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) of the hypothalamus serves as the master circadian clock, coordinating roughly 24-hour rhythms across virtually every organ system. The molecular clockwork relies on a transcription-translation feedback loop involving CLOCK, BMAL1, PER1/2/3, and CRY1/2 proteins. Disruption of this system, whether through shift work, social jetlag, or irregular sleep patterns, has far-reaching consequences for metabolic, immune, and neurological health.

Bass, J., & Lazar, M.A. (2016). Circadian time signatures of fitness and disease. Science, 354(6315), 994-999.
Clock Genes and AD

Circadian Disruption and Neurodegeneration

Musiek and Holtzman (2016) demonstrated that disruption of the BMAL1 gene in mice produces accelerated amyloid-beta accumulation and oxidative damage in the brain, independent of sleep duration. Clock gene disruption impairs glymphatic function, alters the rhythmic expression of amyloid precursor protein, and promotes neuroinflammation. These findings suggest that sleep timing consistency, not just duration, independently influences Alzheimer's risk. Patients with Alzheimer's disease show early disruption of circadian rhythmicity, often manifesting as sundowning syndrome, years before cognitive symptoms become clinically apparent.

Key Insight: Social jetlag (misalignment between internal clock and social schedule) of as little as one hour per day produces measurable increases in inflammatory markers and impairs cognitive performance on standardized tests.
Musiek, E.S., & Holtzman, D.M. (2016). Mechanisms linking circadian clocks, sleep, and neurodegeneration. Science, 354(6315), 1004-1008.
Blue Light

Screen Time, Melatonin, and Sleep Architecture

Short-wavelength (blue) light in the 460 to 480 nm range is the primary suppressor of pineal melatonin secretion via melanopsin-containing intrinsically photosensitive retinal ganglion cells (ipRGCs). Evening screen exposure suppresses melatonin by up to 50 percent and delays sleep onset by 30 to 60 minutes. Chang et al. (2014) found that reading on a light-emitting device before bed suppressed melatonin by 55 percent, delayed the circadian clock by 1.5 hours, and reduced REM sleep the following night. These changes cumulatively reduce slow-wave sleep time and therefore glymphatic clearance efficiency.

Chang, A.M., et al. (2014). Evening use of light-emitting eReaders negatively affects sleep, circadian timing, and next-morning alertness. PNAS, 112(4), 1232-1237.
Adolescent Biology

The Adolescent Circadian Delay

Puberty triggers a biologically driven delay in the circadian phase of approximately 2 hours, causing adolescents to experience sleepiness approximately 2 hours later than pre-pubertal children or adults. This circadian shift, combined with early school start times, produces chronic partial sleep deprivation in the adolescent population. The American Academy of Pediatrics recommends that middle and high schools begin no earlier than 8:30 AM to align with adolescent circadian biology. Chronic sleep restriction during adolescence may have long-term consequences for neural pruning, prefrontal cortex maturation, and potentially the establishment of amyloid clearance patterns.

Carskadon, M.A. (2011). Sleep in adolescents: the perfect storm. Pediatric Clinics of North America, 58(3), 637-647.
Reaction Time

Reaction Time as a Cognitive Biomarker

Simple and choice reaction time tasks are among the most sensitive measures of psychomotor vigilance and processing speed. Intraindividual variability in reaction time (IIV) has emerged as a particularly sensitive index of cognitive decline, with increases in IIV predictive of future dementia diagnosis years before clinical symptoms manifest. Sleep deprivation produces characteristic dose-dependent increases in reaction time and reaction time variability. The Psychomotor Vigilance Task (PVT) developed by Dinges is considered the gold standard for measuring neurobehavioral impairment from sleep loss.

Dinges, D.F., & Powell, J.W. (1985). Microcomputer analyses of performance on a portable, simple visual RT task during sustained operations. Behavior Research Methods, 17(6), 652-655.
Working Memory

Spatial Working Memory and the Hippocampus

Working memory, the capacity to temporarily hold and manipulate information in consciousness, is critically dependent on prefrontal cortex-hippocampal networks. Spatial working memory tasks engage the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex and hippocampus in coordinated activity that is exquisitely sensitive to sleep disruption. Harrison and Horne (2000) showed that 36 hours of sleep deprivation severely impaired innovative thinking and the ability to hold spatial sequences in mind. In the context of Alzheimer's research, working memory capacity correlates inversely with amyloid burden on PET imaging in cognitively normal adults.

Harrison, Y., & Horne, J.A. (2000). The impact of sleep deprivation on decision making. Journal of Sleep Research, 9(1), 49-55.
Pattern Recognition

Executive Function and Pattern Recognition

Pattern recognition and rule induction tasks engage prefrontal executive systems and are sensitive measures of abstract reasoning capacity. Sleep is essential for the consolidation of implicit pattern knowledge; Wagner et al. (2004) showed that a single night of sleep tripled the likelihood that subjects would discover a hidden numerical rule in a cognitive task, demonstrating that sleep facilitates the reorganization and integration of knowledge. Digital pattern recognition tasks are valid, reliable cognitive assessments when administered under standardized conditions, making them suitable for longitudinal home-based monitoring.

Wagner, U., et al. (2004). Sleep inspires insight. Nature, 427(6972), 352-355.
Digital Assessment

Digital Neuropsychological Testing

Digital cognitive assessment has gained substantial validity evidence over the past decade. Wild et al. (2008) reviewed computerized assessment tools for clinical trials and found that digital administration offers superior measurement precision, ecological validity, and sensitivity to subclinical change compared to traditional paper measures. Frequent, brief digital assessments enable the detection of performance trajectories rather than single-point snapshots, which is essential for capturing the relationship between day-to-day sleep variation and cognitive performance. NeuroSleep's cognitive battery is informed by this literature and designed to be sensitive to sleep-related performance fluctuations.

Wild, K., et al. (2008). Computer-assisted cognitive assessment tools in clinical trials. Nature Reviews Drug Discovery, 7(6), 553-556.
Research Design

NeuroSleep Research Methodology

NeuroSleep employs a single-subject longitudinal design, collecting repeated behavioral measures across time to examine within-individual correlations between sleep parameters and cognitive performance outcomes. This design is well-suited to the research question because inter-individual variability in baseline cognitive performance can obscure sleep-cognition relationships in cross-sectional designs. By tracking each user as their own control, NeuroSleep generates n-of-1 correlation data that is meaningful despite a small sample.

Sleep Variables Collected

  • ▶ Sleep duration (hours) computed from bedtime and wake time
  • ▶ Bedtime consistency (standard deviation of bedtime across days)
  • ▶ Subjective sleep quality (1-5 Likert scale)
  • ▶ Screen-to-bed interval (minutes between screen cutoff and bedtime)
  • ▶ Caffeine consumption (estimated daily mg)
  • ▶ Exercise participation (binary)

Cognitive Outcome Variables

  • ▶ Mean reaction time (milliseconds)
  • ▶ Reaction time variability (standard deviation)
  • ▶ Spatial working memory accuracy (percent correct)
  • ▶ Maximum sequence length recalled
  • ▶ Pattern recognition accuracy (percent correct)
  • ▶ Composite cognitive score (weighted average)
Statistical Approach: Pearson correlation coefficients between sleep and cognitive variables; moving averages for trend visualization; color-coded interpretation based on published normative data from sleep deprivation literature.
Risk Modeling

Alzheimer's Risk Score Methodology

The NeuroSleep Alzheimer's Risk Behavior Score is a weighted composite of seven behavioral domains identified in the Lancet 2020 Commission report and subsequent meta-analyses as modifiable predictors of dementia risk. Each domain is scored on a 0 to 35 point subscale, and the total score (0 to 100) places individuals into one of four risk tiers. This tool models behavioral risk factors only and should not be interpreted as a clinical diagnostic measure.

Livingston, G., et al. (2020). Dementia prevention, intervention, and care: 2020 report of the Lancet Commission. The Lancet, 396(10248), 413-446.
Limitations

Methodological Limitations

NeuroSleep acknowledges the following important limitations: (1) Sleep data is self-reported, which introduces recall bias and lacks the precision of polysomnography or actigraphy; (2) Cognitive tests are not clinically validated instruments and may be subject to practice effects over repeated administration; (3) The sample is necessarily limited to individual users, reducing generalizability; (4) The risk calculator does not account for genetic risk factors including APOE e4 genotype, which is the single strongest genetic predictor of late-onset Alzheimer's; (5) Correlation between sleep and cognition in young people does not necessarily predict long-term Alzheimer's risk. These limitations are consistent with the educational and exploratory purpose of this platform.
