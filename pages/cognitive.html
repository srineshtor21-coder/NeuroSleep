About This Test

Reaction Time Measurement

The Psychomotor Vigilance Task (PVT) measures sustained attention and psychomotor speed. It was developed by Dinges (1985) and is the gold standard for assessing neurobehavioral impairment from sleep loss. Reaction time increases significantly with sleep deprivation, often before the individual notices any subjective impairment.

Interpretation: under 220ms = Excellent; 220 to 280ms = Good; 280 to 350ms = Average; above 350ms = Below average for a rested adult.

Spatial Sequence Memory

Level 1

Press Start to begin. Watch the pattern, then reproduce it.

About This Test

Spatial Working Memory

This test measures spatial working memory capacity, a cognitive function critically dependent on prefrontal cortex-hippocampal networks. A sequence of grid cells illuminates in order; you must reproduce the sequence exactly. Each correct reproduction extends the sequence by one step. Sleep deprivation impairs working memory by reducing the functional connectivity between the hippocampus and prefrontal cortex, reducing the maximum sequence length you can retain.

Most rested adults can recall sequences of 5 to 9 items (Miller's Law). Sleep-deprived individuals often show reductions to 3 to 5 items.
How it works
  1. Watch the grid cells light up in sequence
  2. Click them back in the same order
  3. Each correct sequence adds one more step
  4. One wrong click ends the test
  5. Your score is based on the maximum level reached

About This Test

Executive Function Assessment

Pattern recognition and rule induction engage prefrontal executive systems and fluid reasoning capacity. Wagner et al. (2004) demonstrated that sleep facilitates the consolidation of implicit pattern knowledge, with a single night of sleep tripling the likelihood that subjects discover hidden rules. This test assesses the abstract reasoning capacity that is most vulnerable to sleep deprivation-induced prefrontal dysfunction.

Scoring is based on percentage correct out of 10 questions. Well-rested individuals typically score 80 to 100 percent. Sleep-deprived adults often show reduced accuracy on novel rule induction tasks.
Pattern Types Included

  • ▶ Geometric sequences (powers, squares)
  • ▶ Fibonacci-type sequences
  • ▶ Alphabetical sequences
  • ▶ Mixed numerical and symbolic patterns
