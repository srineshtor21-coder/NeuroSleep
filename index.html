The Mechanism

How Sleep Protects Your Brain

Research from Johns Hopkins, the NIH, and the University of Rochester has established a clear mechanistic pathway connecting nightly sleep to Alzheimer's pathology.

→
STEP 01 🛑
Sleep Onset
Brain transitions from wakefulness into NREM slow-wave and REM cycles
→
STEP 02 🖩
Glymphatic Activation
Cerebrospinal fluid surges through perivascular channels, washing the interstitium
→
STEP 03 🧰
Amyloid-Beta Clearance
Metabolic waste proteins including amyloid-beta and tau are flushed from neural tissue
→
STEP 04 🧠
Memory Consolidation
Hippocampal replay transfers declarative memories to long-term cortical storage
STEP 05 ✨
Cognitive Recovery
Synaptic homeostasis restores cognitive bandwidth for next-day learning and performance
Key Finding (Xie et al., 2013, Science): Sleep increases the interstitial space by 60%, allowing CSF to flow at dramatically higher rates and clear amyloid-beta at nearly twice the rate observed during wakefulness. Chronic sleep deprivation was shown to cause a 30% increase in amyloid-beta plaque deposition in mouse models.

Research Areas

Grounded in Evidence-Based Neuroscience

NeuroSleep draws on published research from leading academic medical institutions. The platform's design reflects the current scientific consensus.

01
Alzheimer's Disease

Amyloid & Tau Pathology

The accumulation of amyloid-beta plaques and tau neurofibrillary tangles is the primary pathological hallmark of Alzheimer's. Both are metabolic waste proteins cleared through the glymphatic system during sleep.

Selkoe & Hardy (2016). The amyloid hypothesis of Alzheimer's disease at 25 years. EMBO Molecular Medicine.
02
Sleep Neuroscience

Glymphatic Clearance System

The glymphatic system, first described by Maiken Nedergaard at University of Rochester, is a waste clearance pathway that operates almost exclusively during sleep, removing neurotoxic metabolites from the brain.

Xie et al. (2013). Sleep drives metabolite clearance from the adult brain. Science, 342(6156), 373-377.
03
Cognitive Performance

Sleep Deprivation Effects

Even mild chronic sleep restriction to 6 hours per night produces cognitive deficits equivalent to 24 hours of total sleep deprivation. Working memory, processing speed, and executive function are most acutely affected.

Van Dongen et al. (2003). The cumulative cost of additional wakefulness. Sleep, 26(2), 117-126.
04
Circadian Biology

Clock Gene Disruption

Irregular sleep timing disrupts circadian gene expression, altering BMAL1/CLOCK protein cycles. This chronobiological disruption independently increases Alzheimer's risk beyond sleep duration effects alone.

Musiek & Holtzman (2016). Mechanisms linking circadian clocks, sleep, and neurodegeneration. Science, 354(6315), 1004-1008.
05
Risk Factors

Modifiable Behavioral Risk

The Lancet Commission identifies 12 modifiable risk factors that together account for approximately 40% of worldwide dementia cases. Sleep quality and duration are among the most impactful and accessible to change.

Livingston et al. (2020). Dementia prevention, intervention, and care: 2020 report of the Lancet Commission. The Lancet, 396(10248), 413-446.
06
Assessment

Neuropsychological Measures

Reaction time variability, working memory span, and pattern recognition accuracy are validated, sensitive indicators of early cognitive decline. Digital assessment enables longitudinal tracking that clinical settings often cannot provide.

Wild et al. (2008). Computer-administered cognitive assessment tools in clinical trials. Nature Reviews Drug Discovery, 7(6), 553-556.

About the Project

Research by Mithil Gajula

NeuroSleep was developed as an ISM (Independent Study Mentorship) research project investigating the intersection of sleep science, cognitive performance, and Alzheimer's disease prevention. The platform bridges academic neuroscience with accessible, self-directed behavioral assessment.

The research question at the core of this project: Can longitudinal sleep consistency metrics serve as an early predictor of cognitive decline risk in adolescent and young adult populations?
