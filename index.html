The Amyloid-Beta Connection

Amyloid-beta is a protein fragment produced naturally by the brain. During wakefulness, these fragments accumulate. During sleep, particularly slow-wave (deep) sleep, the glymphatic system activates and cerebrospinal fluid flows through the brain at dramatically increased rates, clearing amyloid-beta and other metabolic waste products. Chronic sleep deprivation disrupts this process, allowing amyloid plaques to build up, which is a hallmark of Alzheimer's disease pathology.

Source: Nature Neuroscience, 2019
🕐

Circadian Rhythm Disruption

The circadian rhythm, your body's internal 24-hour clock, governs hormone release, metabolic activity, and cellular repair. Disruption of this rhythm through irregular sleep schedules, shift work, or chronic late-night screen exposure elevates cortisol, suppresses melatonin production, and impairs the brain's ability to regulate inflammation. Studies show that circadian disruption accelerates tau protein accumulation, another key driver of Alzheimer's neurodegeneration.

Source: NIH National Institute on Aging

Research Foundation

Built on Peer-Reviewed Science

Every feature of NeuroSleep is grounded in published neuroscience research from leading institutions.

01

Glymphatic System Research

Pioneering work by Dr. Maiken Nedergaard at the University of Rochester demonstrated that the brain's waste-clearance system is 10x more active during sleep, removing toxic proteins including amyloid-beta that are central to Alzheimer's progression.

Johns Hopkins, 2019
02

Sleep Consistency Studies

Research published in the Journal of Alzheimer's Disease found that irregular sleep timing, not just duration, is independently associated with cognitive decline. Participants with highly variable sleep schedules showed 27% greater memory impairment.

Journal of Alzheimer's Disease, 2021
03

Tau Protein & Circadian Rhythm

NIH-funded studies at Washington University School of Medicine established that circadian rhythm disruption accelerates tau spread in the brain. Even a single night of sleep deprivation resulted in a 50% increase in tau levels in cerebrospinal fluid.

NIH, Science 2019

About This Project

Created by Mithil Gajula

NeuroSleep is an ISM (Independent Study Mentorship) research project exploring the intersection of neuroscience, sleep medicine, and Alzheimer's prevention through data-driven technology.

The platform serves as a functional research tool that collects real behavioral data, runs statistical analyses, and generates insights grounded in peer-reviewed science. It demonstrates how modifiable lifestyle factors, particularly sleep consistency, can influence measurable cognitive outcomes and long-term neurological risk.

This project is mentored by faculty in neurology and reflects an ongoing commitment to understanding one of the most complex and important challenges in modern medicine.
